Professional Email Structure

01 Purpose

State why you are writing in the very first sentence.

"I am writing to update you on…""I'd like to ask about…"

02 Details

Provide concise, relevant background. Use bullets for lists of items.

03 Action

State what you want the reader to do next.

"Please let me know by…""Let me know your availability"

Sentence Types

Simple

One independent clause

One complete idea. Great for emphasis and clarity. Too many in a row sound choppy.

"Grammar matters."
Compound

Two independent clauses

Joined by FANBOYS: For And Nor But Or Yet So. Must have a comma before the conjunction.

"Grammar is hard, but I will master it."
Complex

Independent + dependent clause

Joined by subordinating conjunction (if, because, although, when). Comma needed only when dependent clause comes FIRST.

"When I study, I do well."

Common Sentence Errors

Fragment

A group of words that lacks a subject, verb, or complete thought.

Wrong
"Which is why we have midterms."
Fixed
"The semester is halfway over, which is why we have midterms."

Fused run-on

Two complete ideas crammed together with no punctuation.

Wrong
"The experiment failed it was left unobserved."
Fixed
"The experiment failed; it was left unobserved."

Comma splice

Two independent clauses joined by only a comma — no conjunction.

Wrong
"Grammar is hard, I hate it."
Fixed — add conjunction
"Grammar is hard, so I hate it." OR use a semicolon.

Punctuation Quick Reference

MarkKey ruleExample
CommaBefore FANBOYS in compound sentences; after introductory dependent clause; around parenthetical info; in lists (Oxford comma)"I study, but I struggle."
SemicolonConnects two closely related complete sentences. Can fix a comma splice."Grammar is hard; it takes practice."
ColonAfter a complete sentence to introduce a list, example, or quote. NOT after "including" or "such as"."You need three things: time, effort, and focus."
ApostrophePossession (noun+'s) OR contraction. Never for plurals. its = belongs to it; it's = it is."The company's policy; it's clear."

Common Word Confusion

their / there / they're

  • Their = belonging to them
  • There = place or to introduce a clause
  • They're = they are

its / it's

  • Its = belonging to it
  • It's = it is / it has

then / than

  • Than = comparison ("better than")
  • Then = time sequence ("first… then")

affect / effect

  • Affect = verb (to influence)
  • Effect = noun (the result); rarely a verb meaning "to cause"

Other Grammar Traps

Subject-verb agreement

The subject and verb must match in number. Watch out for phrases between subject and verb that trick you.

Wrong
"Various kinds of vandalism has been increasing."
Correct
"Various kinds of vandalism have been increasing."

Misplaced modifier

A word/phrase that accidentally modifies the wrong thing. Move it next to what it describes.

Wrong
"At the age of three, he caught a fish with a broken arm."
Fixed
"At the age of three, the boy with a broken arm caught a fish."

Dangling participle

The participial phrase modifies a noun that isn't actually in the sentence.

Wrong
"Having finished the assignment, the TV was turned on."
Fixed
"Having finished the assignment, Jill turned on the TV."

Unclear pronoun ("this", "it", "they")

Always add a specific noun after "this" so readers know what it refers to.

Unclear
"The system enforces MFA. This reduces risk."
Clear
"The system enforces MFA. This enforcement reduces risk."

Report Design Principles

Font

  • Serif (Times New Roman) for print
  • Sans-serif (Arial) for digital
  • 11–12pt body; larger for headings
  • Bold headings; bold/italic for emphasis

White space & margins

  • 1-inch margins
  • Single-space within paragraphs
  • One blank line between paragraphs
  • White space around visuals

Headings

  • Always bold
  • Informative and specific (not just "Introduction")
  • Major headings = larger font
  • Use Word Styles for consistency

Visuals

  • Label as "Table 1" or "Figure 1" with a descriptive title
  • Introduce every visual in body text before it appears
  • Visuals complement text; they don't replace it

The 4 Rhetorical Appeals (Elements of Persuasion)

Ethos

Credibility & Authority

  • Highlight your expertise and qualifications
  • Use credible, cited sources
  • Demonstrate professional grammar and organization
Pathos

Emotion, Interest, Values

  • Show alignment with audience values and org goals
  • Use real-world impacts, storytelling, case studies
  • Build common ground
Logos

Logic & Reason

  • Provide facts, data, statistics
  • Anticipate counterarguments
  • Acknowledge limitations honestly
  • Use effective structure (direct vs. indirect)
Kairos

Timeliness & Appropriateness

  • Emphasize urgency — why act NOW?
  • Connect to current trends and policies
  • Align with institutional goals

Research & References

Primary sources

Research you do yourself: surveys, interviews, experiments, observations, company records.

Secondary sources

Analysis/interpretation of others' work: journal articles, books, government documents, news articles.

(Author Last Name, Year) — e.g., (Smith, 2021)

Document: ideas, quotations, paraphrases, images, data, AI-generated content. When in doubt, cite it.

  • What tasks it helped with (brainstorming, editing, writing, images)

  • Extent of your edits to the AI output

Writing Effective Instructions

Structure

  • Introduction (purpose, background, materials, prerequisites, safety warnings)
  • Numbered chronological steps
  • Conclusion (expected results, outcome)

Audience

  • Always assume reader knows less than you
  • State expected knowledge level
  • Define specialized terms
  • Give enough examples to visualize the procedure

Key Writing Rules for Steps

  • Begin each step with an action verb (imperative mood)
  • One step per numbered item; one action per step
  • Use active voice: "Key in your access code" not "The access code is keyed in"
  • Use affirmative phrasing: "Examine your CD for dust" not "Verify it is not contaminated"
  • Use parallel phrasing — identical grammatical form for all steps
  • Use transition words for sequence: next, then, after, before, finally

Safety Notices (in order of severity)

Note

Clarifies a point, describes options or alternatives.

Caution

Prevents mistakes that could result in injury or equipment damage.

Warning

Alerts users against hazards to life or limb.

Danger

Identifies an immediate hazard to life or limb.

Scenario / Application
Your introduction should include:
1. Purpose — explain what task the instructions accomplish
2. Prerequisite knowledge — state what the reader should already know
3. Materials/equipment list — tire levers, patch kit, pump, new tube
4. Safety notices — if applicable (e.g., release air pressure before removing)
5. Definitions — define specialized terms (e.g., "valve stem," "bead")
